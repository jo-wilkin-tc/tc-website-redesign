Air Quality

The air Californians breathe — measured neighborhood by neighborhood

Air pollution remains one of California's most significant environmental health threats. Despite decades of progress under the Clean Air Act, millions of residents still live in areas that exceed federal standards for fine particulate matter (PM2.5) and ozone — and the burden is far from evenly shared.

Tracking California compiles regulatory monitor readings, modeled estimates, and community-collected measurements to show how air quality varies across the state, down to the census-tract level. The data reveals patterns that statewide averages hide: pollution concentrated along freight corridors, near ports and rail yards, and in agricultural valleys where geography traps emissions.

Why it matters

Poor air quality is linked to asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), heart attacks, low birth weight, and premature death. The communities most exposed are frequently those with the fewest resources to respond — low-income neighborhoods and communities of color situated near industrial and transportation sources.

The equity gap is real. In parts of the San Joaquin Valley and the Los Angeles freight corridor, residents experience PM2.5 exposure that would be considered a public health emergency if it occurred statewide. Mapping exposure alongside health outcomes is the first step toward targeting action where it's needed most.

Key findings

  • Roughly 1 in 3 Californians live in a census tract that exceeded the annual PM2.5 standard at least once in the past five years.
  • Asthma emergency-department visit rates are up to twice as high in the most polluted tracts compared with the cleanest.
  • Wildfire smoke now accounts for a growing share of annual PM2.5 exposure, reversing gains made through emissions controls.
  • Neighborhoods within 500 meters of major freeways show consistently elevated pollutant levels regardless of regional air-basin averages.
