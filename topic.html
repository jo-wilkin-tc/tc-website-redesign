Air Quality

Tracking California compiles regulatory monitor readings, modeled estimates, and community-collected measurements to show how air quality varies across the state, down to the census-tract level. Statewide averages hide patterns such as pollution concentrated along freight corridors, near ports and rail yards, and in agricultural valleys where geography traps emissions.

Fine particulate matter (PM2.5), ozone, and traffic-related pollutants are linked to asthma, chronic respiratory and cardiovascular disease, low birth weight, and premature death. We map exposure alongside health outcomes so communities can see where the burden falls and where action is needed.

Traffic-related exposure is a particular focus: neighborhoods near major freeways, ports, and freight routes face elevated pollutant levels that regional air-basin averages can mask.

The most exposed communities are often low-income neighborhoods and communities of color situated near industrial and transportation sources. Official monitoring networks leave gaps in exactly these places, so combining regulatory data with community-collected measurements is essential to target action fairly.

Key findings

1 in 3 Californians

Live in a census tract that exceeded the annual PM2.5 standard at least once in the past five years.

2x asthma ED visits

Asthma emergency-department visit rates run up to twice as high in the most polluted tracts versus the cleanest.

Smoke reversing gains

Wildfire smoke now drives a growing share of annual PM2.5, offsetting decades of emissions controls.
