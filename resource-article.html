What community-collected air-quality data reveals that official monitors miss

By Dr. Maya Reyes, Tracking California · March 2026

California's regulatory air-quality monitors are precise, calibrated, and used to track whether the air meets federal standards. But there are only a few hundred of them across a state of nearly 40 million people, and they were not designed to describe the air on a single block, outside a school, or at the fence line of a facility.

Community-collected data helps fill that gap. Residents in places like Oxnard, Wilmington, and the eastern Coachella Valley have deployed dense networks of low-cost sensors — sometimes dozens within a few square miles. The readings are noisier than a regulatory monitor's and need correction before they can be trusted, but together they reveal patterns a sparse official network cannot see.

Pollution is local, and so is the data that describes it

Air quality varies widely over short distances. A regulatory monitor a few miles upwind of a freight corridor may report air that meets the standard, while a sensor two blocks from the trucks records spikes several times higher during the morning rush. Neither reading is wrong; they are measuring different air. Only one reflects what a child at a nearby bus stop is breathing.

Why this matters: When a community can show that exposure on their block is consistently higher than the nearest official monitor suggests, the burden of proof shifts. The question is no longer "is there a problem?" but "what are we going to do about it?"

Community sensors also capture the timing of pollution that monthly or annual averages obscure. Short, intense spikes — a flaring event, a line of idling trucks, an overnight inversion that traps smoke near the ground — can dominate a resident's exposure even when the long-run average looks unremarkable. Dense, continuous local data makes those episodes visible.

Figure 1. Hourly PM2.5 from a community sensor network (orange) compared with the nearest regulatory monitor (blue) over one week. The official monitor smooths over the morning and evening spikes that residents experience.

The value isn't just the numbers — it's who collected them

When residents gather the data themselves, they understand it and are ready to act on it. Placing a sensor, checking its readings, and comparing notes with neighbors turns abstract risk into something concrete. Data that people helped produce is data they are willing to bring to a city council meeting, a school board, or a regulator.

Our role at Tracking California is not to replace the regulatory network, which remains the reference for what it was built to do. It is to help communities collect credible local data, correct and contextualize it against reference monitors, and put it into tools that make the patterns legible. The official numbers tell us whether a region complies; the community numbers tell us where people are being harmed. Both are needed.

Where this goes next

The next step is making community data durable: sustaining sensor networks beyond a single grant cycle, building calibration pipelines that keep low-cost readings reliable, and connecting air-quality observations to health outcomes such as asthma visits and missed school days. None of that happens without the residents who keep the sensors running. The task now is to make sure decision-makers are listening.
