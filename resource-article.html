What community-collected air-quality data reveals that official monitors miss

By Dr. Maya Reyes, Tracking California · March 2026

California has one of the most extensive air-quality monitoring networks in the country. Regulatory monitors run by air districts and the state are precise, carefully calibrated, and essential for tracking whether the air meets federal standards. But there are only a few hundred of them across a state of nearly 40 million people — and they were never designed to describe what the air is like on a single block, outside a single school, or at the fence line of a single facility.

That gap is exactly where community-collected data has begun to change the conversation. Over the past several years, residents in places like Oxnard, Wilmington, and the eastern Coachella Valley have deployed dense networks of low-cost sensors — sometimes dozens within a few square miles. The readings are noisier than a regulatory monitor's, and they need careful correction before they can be trusted. But taken together, they reveal patterns that a sparse official network simply cannot see.

Pollution is local, and so is the data that describes it

The clearest lesson from community monitoring is that air quality varies enormously over short distances. A regulatory monitor sited a few miles upwind of a busy freight corridor may report air that comfortably meets the standard, while a sensor two blocks from the trucks records spikes several times higher during the morning rush. Neither reading is wrong. They are simply measuring different air — and only one of them reflects what a child waiting at a nearby bus stop is actually breathing.

Why this matters: When a community can show that exposure on their block is consistently higher than the nearest official monitor suggests, the burden of proof shifts. The question is no longer "is there a problem?" but "what are we going to do about it?"

Community sensors also capture the timing of pollution in ways that monthly or annual averages obscure. Short, intense spikes — a flaring event, a backed-up line of idling trucks, an overnight inversion that traps smoke near the ground — can dominate a resident's real exposure even when the long-run average looks unremarkable. Dense, continuous local data makes those episodes visible, and visibility is the first step toward accountability.

Figure 1. Hourly PM2.5 from a community sensor network (orange) compared with the nearest regulatory monitor (blue) over one week. The official monitor smooths over the morning and evening spikes that residents experience.

The value isn't just the numbers — it's who collected them

There is a second, quieter lesson here. When residents gather the data themselves, they understand it, trust it, and are ready to act on it in a way that no outside report can replicate. The act of placing a sensor, checking its readings, and comparing notes with neighbors turns abstract risk into something concrete and shared. Data that people helped produce is data they are willing to bring to a city council meeting, a school board, or a regulator.

Our role at Tracking California is not to replace the regulatory network — it remains the gold standard for what it was built to do. Our role is to help communities collect credible local data, correct and contextualize it against reference monitors, and put it into tools that make the patterns legible. The official numbers tell us whether a region complies. The community numbers tell us where people are actually being harmed. We need both.

Where this goes next

The next frontier is making community data durable: sustaining sensor networks beyond a single grant cycle, building the calibration pipelines that keep low-cost readings trustworthy, and connecting air-quality observations to the health outcomes — asthma visits, missed school days — that give them weight. None of that happens without the residents who keep the sensors running. The data they collect is already telling us things the official network never could. The task now is to make sure decision-makers are listening.
